Summary of key findings
Eight findings from data
01
Claude is the preferred primary tool among DS and AI students despite ChatGPT having broader adoption
Source: primary tool distribution. 13 of 62 students list Claude as their primary tool, a disproportionate share relative to its total user count of 43. This gap reveals that many ChatGPT and Gemini users treat those tools as ambient utilities while reserving genuine preference for Claude.
02
Clear tool to task affinity exists and differs meaningfully across tools
Source: use case heatmap. Claude users index toward writing and research. ChatGPT shows a flat affinity profile across all tasks, confirming its generalist role. GitHub Copilot concentrates almost entirely on coding. Tool choice and task choice are correlated but the alignment is imperfect.
03
ChatGPT functions as the universal on-ramp into AI tool adoption
Source: Sankey migration analysis. 46 of 62 students used ChatGPT as their first AI tool. Every migration path passes through it. The dominant exit route from ChatGPT is toward Claude (12 switchers), followed by Gemini (8 switchers). This suggests a maturation arc where sustained AI users eventually differentiate their toolset.
04
High usage intensity and high concern about skill atrophy coexist without contradiction
Source: correlation analysis. Students who use AI most frequently also report the highest concern about skill atrophy. The assumption that heavy users are uncritically dependent is refuted. The most active users appear simultaneously most aware of what they may be trading away.
05
Specialist tools earn disproportionately high trust relative to their user count
Source: trust vs utility scatter. GitHub Copilot and Cursor sit in the high-trust, high-utility quadrant. Their outputs are immediately verifiable: code either compiles and runs or it does not. General purpose LLMs cluster in the middle despite their dominance because their outputs are harder to verify without domain expertise.
06
Perplexity is the trust outlier: saves hours but is distrusted
Source: trust vs utility scatter. Perplexity saves a comparable number of hours to the general LLM cluster but carries the lowest average trust score. Students use it for research and summarization but question whether its citations are accurate. High utility with low verification ability produces this specific distrust pattern.
07
AI generated content submission without attribution is structural, not an edge case
Source: ethics question. Only 18% of respondents say they have never submitted AI generated content without significant modification or citation. 32% say often or sometimes. Given voluntary self-reporting, actual rates are likely higher. This is a systemic academic integrity pattern, not an outlier behavior.
08
Tool choice is partially predictable from behavior but Claude vs ChatGPT preference is behaviorally indistinct
Source: decision tree classification. The model achieves 0.567 cross-validated accuracy against a 0.33 baseline. However, Claude has an F1 of only 0.48 because Claude users and ChatGPT users share nearly identical use case profiles. What differentiates them is output quality perception and writing style preference, both of which lie outside survey-measurable space.
Acknowledged limitations
Sample skews toward second year DS and AI students at IIT Bhilai. Not representative of all IIT students or all engineering students without a larger stratified sample.
N = 62 is sufficient for exploratory analysis but insufficient for robust predictive modeling. All accuracy metrics should be interpreted as directional, not definitive.
Hours saved is self-reported and subject to estimation bias. Students likely overestimate time savings.
Ethics responses are subject to social desirability bias. Actual rates of unmodified AI submission are likely higher than reported.
